INTRODUCTION

Scotland has been a country of outward migration for many years, with over 2.3 million
people leaving Scotland between 1825 and 1938. (Scottish Government, 2021). In
Scotland, the inhabitants of the island continued to migrate to mainland. In August 1930,
the remaining 36 inhabitants of St Kilda were leave to the Scottish mainland, and since
then the island has no more settled citizens (Sefari, 2021). There are currently 96
inhabited islands in Scotland, and there are many other islands that are nothing but ruins.
Thus, in 1965, Willie Ross, the Scottish Secretary of State, delivered a speech in the
House of Commons announcing the establishment of the Highlands and Islands
Development Board (HIDB), one of the signs that the Scottish Government was beginning
to take the people of the islands seriously (Sefari, 2021). In 2018, the Scottish
Government developed the National Islands Plan to promote the voices of island
communities, harnessing island resources; This is to Enhance the allocations of island
communities (Scotland Government, 2018), many islands may thus be reborn (Glass, J,
2020).

This paper takes Arran Island as an example to study the economic decline of Arran Island
after the epidemic. It is assumed that the government has formulated different strategies
to stimulate the island after the epidemic, and SMCA (Social Multi-Criteria) is used to
analyze different alternations to get the final results.

BACKGROUND TO THE STUDY

Environment of Arran Island

Arran is one of the southernmost islands in the Scottish archipelago. The boundary
between the Highlands and Lowlands of Scotland almost divides the island in two parts,
so Arran Island more broadly reflects Scotland’s different landscapes and has been
dubbed as “miniature Scotland”. The mountainous, sparsely populated in north and the
flat south are home to most of the 4,600-odd islanders, making it Scotland’s the sixthmost-populated island.

On the animal side, the local group, the Aran Seabed Trust, has worked to make the
island’s waters Scotland’s a exclusive economic zone, which prohibits destructive fishing
activities such as dredging and trawling. The ecology of the land is also well preserved,
where visitors can find red squirrels, deer, golden eagles, otters, seals and other wildlife.

SOCIAL CHARACTERISTICS OF ARRAN ISLAND

History, art and religion

Arran Island has a long history and has been inhabited since the Stone Age, which dating
back to 7000 BC. Machrie marsh standing stones are strong evidence of its long history,
dating from between 3500 and 1500BC, some of which are over 5 m high. Arran Island
also has a strong artistic scene. There are galleries and museums on the island. The
galleries have a collection of Scottish contemporary artists, while the museums preserve
the most original social history of Arran Island.

The Island’s Lamlash Bay offers spectacular coastal views of Holy Island, a Holy place for
the Irish Church but also home to many Buddhist retreats, just a mile away from Arran
Island (Center for world peace and health, 2021).

Transportation:

The only way to get to the island is by ferry, and there are two routes. The more famous
route from Ardrossan to Brodick (the name of a village on the island). Just taking 45
minutes from Glasgow, Ardrossan is an easy destination for hikers, geologists and nature
lovers from all over the country.

There are only two roads on the island, a 55-mile road that wraps around the island’s
shoreline and a road that cuts through the center of the island known locally as “the
strings.” Almost all of Arran’s settlements and many of its attractions are located on or
near the island’s main roads, and buses there are the main means of transportation for
travelers once on the island (Transport Scotland,2020,).

ECONOMIC CHARACTERISTICS OF ARRAN ISLAND

In 2019, the Isle of Arran had an estimated of 4,600 inhabitants. Some 2,500 of these
residents are of working age (16-64) and 2,000 are employed. According to the business
register and employment survey, a quarter of all employment is concentrated in the
accommodation and food services sector, retail 11 per cent, transport 9 per cent and
entertainment 6 per cent, and all four major sectors continue to be driven by tourism.

WHY I CHOOSE ARRAN ISLAND?

According to the figure 2, the outbreak took a serious hit on the GDP of Arran Island,
especially when the lockdown was first announced in March and April of 2020. In
addition, the tourism industry almost stagnated during the epidemic period, and a large
number of layoffs in the related accommodation and catering industries led to an
increase in the unemployment rate of the islanders, and the unemployment situation of
the population aged 16-24 was partiucularly serious (BBC, 2020). The demographic
change caused by the decrease in the youth population was extremely detrimental to
population growth.

So for Arran Island, it’s going to need external policies to changethe economic situation in
order to quickly return to the pre-epidemic situation. As a result, many merchants were
out of business during the outbreak, and they continued to attract tourists to Arran
Island. The research of this project is based on the assumption that the local government
of Arran Island has taken measures, so we can compare different alternatives through
SMCA which is best suited to save the current situation of Arran Island.

LITERATURE REVIEW

SMCA ((Social Multi-Criteria))
This project involves the comparison of different solutions under different criteria, not
only in terms of money, but also in terms of the values, aspirations, and motivations of
the stakeholders. As a diversified evaluation method, SMCA takes advantage of the weak
comparability of internal values to solve the disadvantages of value incommensurability
and technology incommensurability, Munda(2004) (Martinez-Alier et al.(1998).
Moreover, in SMCA, different stakeholders can negotiate to find a compromise scheme
to achieve a balance at the technical and social levels (Munda(2004). At the same time,
the more participants involved in the project, the more transparency of the review will be
improved, the results will be better, and democracy and decentralization will be
promoted (Norgaard, 1989, p. 52).

AHP
Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) (Saaty, 1982, Saaty, 1988, Saaty, 1995) is probably the
best known and most widely used MCA method. It is based on three principles,
namely :(1) hierarchy construction, (2) priority setting, and (3) logical consistency. It is
simple to structure the problem, divide the project into standards and alternatives, and
determine different priorities by comparison ( Macharis, 2004). The essence of AHP is
that it is possible to use human judgment rather than just basic information to perform
an estimate (Forman, 2001).

STAKEHOLDER

BUSINESS ASSOCIATIONS

Business Associations are kinds of private sector stakeholder, while the Arran Island
Business Associations are represented by the association of golf clubs. There are seven
golf courses on Arran Island, including the only 11-hole golf course in Europe. All of them
belong to the primary Business association, and the tickets purchased by customers are
universal in all the courses. Many visitors are drawn to Arran Island by its diverse
selection of golf courses, so the Golf Business Association makes a significant
contribution to the economy of Arran Island and provides a tax base for public services.
And it can coordinate the interests of internal members, has a strong pricing power for
the whole market.

INDIVIDUAL BUSINESSES

Individual businesses are Private Sector stakeholders. For Arran Island, the development
of self-employment is very diversified. For example, an ice cream shop and cheese shop,
which uses the milk from the Arran Island ranch that has been around for more than 20
years. The distillery that once gave Queen Elizabeth whisky, and the newly built brewery.
And the Arran Island pattern of the woolen garment factory. But in fact, most of these
merchants sell to tourists outside the whisky distillery, which is highly tied to the tourism
industry. In addition to being businessmen, they are mostly residents of Arran Island and
therefore relatively conscious of the environment, thatis why tourists come. These retail
businesses also account for a large proportion of the total GDP, and they also contribute
to the diversification of the island’s industries, which should be considered in the analysis
project.

GENERAL PUBLIC

The general public stakeholder is a public sector stakeholder.
The general public here, especially those who have grown up and lived on Arran Island
for a long time, need to be distinguished from outsiders. They care about the benefits
they received, and immigration squeezes them. For example, when fresh water is scarce
in summer, the water supply is insufficient due to the increase of outsiders, which affects
their lives (Home energy Scotland). And young people among them may choose to leave
the islands in pursuit of a better life.

OUTSIDER

Outsiders are public sector stakeholders. However, different from the general public,
Outsiders do not necessarily settle in Arran Island. They may only appear on holidays. In
some villages in Arran, about 40% of the houses are used for holidays, leading to a
shortage of rental housing. Therefore, they do not need pay as much attention to the
economic development and ecological environment of the island as ordinary residents. It
needs to be considered because some of their interests are in conflict with ordinary
residents, because their entry will lead to the rise of the island housing prices. Moreover,
they may not care about the infrastructure construction of the island, because they do
not need to live on the island for a long time. Therefore, although they share the same
interests with the general public in some aspects, they do not care about it to the same
degree.

LOCAL AUTHORITY

The local authority is the most representative of the public sector. Compared with the
central government, it is directly responsible for the local management, close to the
interests of the people and local enterprises, and will safeguard the public interests and
the well-being of the residents. Now the authorities on Arran Island need to think about
how to stimulate economic recovery and how to draw tourists. And thinking about how
to make the Arran Island economy more resilient.

SOCIAL ENTERPRISES AND ADVOCACY GROUPS

Social enterprises and advocacy groups are a kind of civil society stakeholders. There are
several Social enterprises and advocacy groups on the island, such as the Alan Undersea
Trust, which works to protect Marine life such as corals, and Arran Eco Savvy, which
promotes solar energy (Arran Eco Savvy, 2020). They are well aware of the importance of
protecting the environment, using renewable energy and improving the energy efficiency
of their homes, so they tend to put the environment at the top of their policy agenda.

CRITERIA

Criterion 1 – c1 – Population
Sub-criterion 1 (c1a) : Avoid population decline and strive for population growth.
Most of the islands belong to the Spa Region, and by Spa Region we mean… It covers
about half the area of Scotland and accounts for 48.8%, but has 2.6% of the population.
And as people continue to move away from the islands for finding jobs and education,
Scotland’s population as a whole grew by 9% between 1991 and 2011, while the SPA
population fell by 2%. So it can be argued that population decline is a real threat to the
sustainability of most of Scotland’s island communities. In the past decade, the number
of islands that have lost people almost doubled. The decrease of population leads to the
decrease of labor force, which promotes the economic depression of the island and traps
it into a vicious circle. (s,2018)

**Sub-criterion 1 (c1b) **: Improvement of population age structure ensures a healthy,
balanced population profile.

The key demographic problem in sparsely populated areas is not too many old people
who live there, but relatively few children and young people. According to projections,
the working population will drop to 25 percent by 2037 if the local government does not
take action. So an appropriate age structure of the population is very crucial. (s, 2020)

Criterion 2 – c2 – Housing and fuel

Sub-criterion 1 (c2a)： Addresses the housing needs of the island

In the early 2000s, Arran Island was similar to North Ayrshire and Scotland. But so many
tourists go to Arran Island to buy real estate that 23 percent of the island’s homes are
vacation homes. The high number of homes occupied by tourists has pushed up prices,
with Arran Island’s house price almost double the average in North Ayrshire in 2018. The
average annual salary on Arran Island is £24,000, but the average house price is eight to
ten times of that. And because of transportation costs and distance, building a house on
its islands is often more complex and difficult than mainland areas, so the cost of building
a house has increased by at least 30 to 40 percent.

Sub-criterion 1 (c2b) : sufficient energy

The main energy consumption on Arran Island is fossil energy, and the main energy
supplier is SSE. Electricity consumption is 28 percent, and 80 percent is transmitted
through submarine cables. The wood fuel is obtained by companies on the island that cut
sustainable renewable forests. Most island authorities have higher rates of extreme fuel
poverty (from 18 per cent in the Shetland Islands to 25 per cent in Syrle, Nah-Ellandia, in
2015-17) than Scotland as a whole (12 per cent).

Criterion 3 – c3 – Environmental well-being and biosecurity

Sub-criterion 1 (c3a): Protect the environment and historical sites

There are two 19th century castles on Arran Island which are the main historical and
cultural heritage, and the unique and beautiful scenery on Arran Island which draw
tourists. Therefore, attention needs to be paid to reducing environmental damage to
both industrial production and transportation. A healthy environment is also vital to
Scotland’s ability to respect, protect and achieve human rights

Sub-criterion 2 (c3b): Protect biodiversity and enhance biosecurity

The island’s ecosystem is fragile, so alien species are likely to affect the biodiversity of
Arran Island. Biosafety needs to be addressed in a holistic and integrated way, not only as
a means of promoting environmental well-being, but also as a means of promoting
sustainable economic development in the Scottish Isles, says the Islands report.

Criterion 4 – c4 – Social welfare

**Sub-criterion 4 (c4a): **Physical health improvement

To provide high quality health and social care services to the residents of the Scottish
islands, doctors and surgeons need to travel to the mainland, which may causes worry
and stress for many older families or those with young children, and the cost of hospital
travel is not affordable for everyone.

Sub-criterion 4 (c4b): Mental health improvement

Scotland government should protect mental health and promotes spiritual
companionship. Many children in the island lack the company of their parents and peers
to play, and many retired elderly people also lack psychological care, so the community
and the island need to pay attention to these people (Samman, E., et al. 2016).

Criterion 5 – c5 – To achieve economic growth

Sub-criterion 5 (c5a): Achieve sustainable development

The economy of Arran Island has been badly hit. Many shops and businesses have not
opened for business outside the island for nearly a year. Residents and business owners
may have very little savings left, so it is necessary to make a profit after the closure is
lifted. However, sustainable development should be achieved at the same time. For
example, for some islands that exploit fossil energy, it is a good plan to set up a trust for
foreign investment.

**Sub-criterion 5 (c5b): ((improve the diversity of business behavior, improve the
ability to see risks

When the island industry is too monolithic, it is less resilient to takerisk. As far as Arran
Island is concerned, its industries are too dependent on tourism. It can try to diversify its
development and increase the distribution channels of products in the island. Products
such as whisky and beer can be tried to find agents to sell in other countries.

Criterion 6 – c6 – Communication and exchange with the outside of the island

Sub-criterion 6 (c6a): improves transportation efficiency

Islands should increase their opportunities to communicate with the outside world while
considering their carbon emissions. At the same time, it can build the basic highway in
the island to improve the transportation comfort and efficiency of the islanders.

Sub-criterion 6 (c6b): establishes a stable digital connection
In the north of Arran Island, 6% of residents don’t have a digital connection.So digital
connectivity is important for islanders, but it could also facilitate other industries, such as
education and health care, such as remote classes or remote consultations.

ALTERNATIVES

Alternative 1 – A1 – Business as usual.

Arran Island continues to develop a sustainable tourism industry and events to attract
visitors to Arran Island. Because of the recent lifting of the ban, there is likely to be a
surge in visitors to Arran Island, so additional boat trips may be needed to better
promote visitors to the island. The increase in tourists and the frequency of ships may
have an impact on the environment.

Alternative 2 – A2 – Loan and develop new energy sources.

The Bank of Scotland lent money at low interest to the local government on Arran Island,
which used the money to build hydroelectric and wind power stations. In 2016, the Bank
of Scotland lent money to build a hydroelectric dam on Allen Island (BBC, 2016). The dam
can also be used to store water to avoid water shortages for islanders during the dry
season. At the same time, the construction of hydropower stations and other facilities
need to recruit workers, which can promote the employment of people who lost their
jobs during the epidemic and increase the employment rate of islanders. And when
islanders get jobs, they start spending, starting with retail and other industries.
Moreover, when the infrastructure of Arran Island is improved, tourists will be promoted
to spend money on Arran Island, so that the government will get more tax from it, and
the increased tax can be used to repay the loan of the Bank of Scotland.

Alternative 3 – A3 – Lower rates.

Assuming the central government exempts Arran Island producers from the tax, with
little change in pricing, this could lead to higher profits for retailers on the island,
encouraging them and their upstream manufacturers to expand, perhaps by opening
new factories for whisky distilleries, for example. And when the tax is reduced, the unit
price of goods may also be reduced, which can promote consumer consumption.
Meanwhile, on Arran Island, the increase in sales of cheese and ice cream parlors may
encourage the livestock industry to expand. As these industries expand, they may affect
the local environment.

PREPARATION OF THE ANALYSIS

WEAK COMPARABILITY AND OUTRANKING STRATEGY

The criteria analysed and compared in this project are of different dimensions, values
that are not commensurable from the perspective of ecological economics (MartinezAlier, J et al, 1998). For different stakeholders on Arran Island, they have different
stances, values and priorities (Zhang, 2018, Mori and Christodoulou,
2012). Therefore, it is impossible for different standards to substitute each other.
Therefore, we assume that social and technical are weakly comparative, and use SMCA to
evaluate and deal with them.

For the outing strategy, this project meets the following assumptions.

  1. The outing strategy should base on pairwise comparisons of alternatives. If any
    alternative appears as the ‘worst’ for two or more sub-criteria, it is eliminated from
    consideration, and the remaining alternatives re-assessed to see which alternative have
    more “best” performance for any of the criteria. If this rule cannot be applied, then the
    alternative with the most ‘worst performances’ for the sub-criteria will be eliminated
    from consideration.

  2. For the Phase II analysis, if any alternative is ranked as 3rd overall by 3 or more
    stakeholders, then it would be removed from consideration and the remaining ranks
    adjusted.

IMPACT MATRIX RULES

The project’s impact matrix was filled with qualitative values: very bad, bad, moderate,
good, very good. For all criteria, if its impact was stronger, its value would be better. And
as for why we didn’t use quantitative response, because it can’t provide a sufficient data
for the respective alternatives.

NEGOTIATION AND PHASE II RECOMMENDATIONS

For the results of the second stage, A2 is undoubtedly the best, because the most people
put it in the first place, and the least people put it in the third place.
People can divide the six stakeholders into three camps. Outsider supported A1, General
Public, Local Authority and Social Enterprises and Advocacy Groups supported A2, and
Business Association supported A3. Individual business.
It can be found that most stakeholders of civil society and public sector support A2,
because A2 project promotes consumption through the construction of new energy
plants. At the same time, it also builds urban infrastructure and renewable new energy
facilities. Those who choose A3 are representatives of the private sector, because A3
directly reduces the tax of the production plant, saves the cash flow of businessmen,
improves the conversion rate, and is conducive to reproduction and expansion of
production capacity. However, the potential risk is that it is easy to affect the
environment. As for outsider, the construction of the new energy plant had few benefits
for them, because they were only on the island during holidays, and the wind power
plant would also affect the scenery on the island. And the policy of lowering taxes,
because they were not merchants on the island, was very good for them. Therefore, they
tend to choose A1, which brings joy to their life.
